## THE 13 CAUSES OF ACTION

The court notes the original state court complaint alleged **13 state-law causes of action**. While we don't have the full complaint text (it's referenced as ECF No. 1-1), this is an unusually high number of claims — suggesting extensive allegations of misconduct, not a simple contract dispute. Typical causes of action in a 13-count crypto mining complaint would include:
- Breach of contract (multiple counts for multiple agreements)
- Fraud / fraudulent misrepresentation
- Negligent misrepresentation
- Breach of fiduciary duty
- Unjust enrichment
- Conversion
- Civil conspiracy
- Unfair trade practices (SC state law)
- Breach of warranty
- Accounting

**This is NOT a simple breach of contract case.** 13 counts against 8 defendants spanning 4 countries (USA, Canada, UAE, UK) — this was a major international crypto mining dispute.

## HOW THIS HELPS NRG BLOOM v TON

### 1. Pattern of Conduct (STRONGEST USE)
Tyler has a documented prior history of entering multi-party crypto mining arrangements that end in formal litigation with 13+ causes of action. This is the **exact same pattern** as the NRG Bloom dispute. It rebuts any argument from TON that the circumvention was accidental, a misunderstanding, or isolated.

### 2. Aggravated / Punitive Damages
Canadian courts award punitive damages when conduct is "malicious, oppressive and high-handed" (Whiten v. Pilot Insurance, $1M; Baker v. Blue Cross 2023, $1.5M). A **prior pattern of the same conduct** makes the aggravated damages argument significantly stronger. Tyler cannot claim he didn't know his behavior was wrongful when he was previously sued for similar conduct.

### 3. Duty of Honest Performance (Bhasin v. Hrynew)
The SCC held that ALL commercial contracts carry a duty of honest performance. Evidence that Tyler was previously sued for breach of contract in a crypto mining venture — and then entered ANOTHER crypto mining venture (NRG Bloom/TON) with the same kind of partner exclusion pattern — supports the argument that he knowingly breached the duty of honest performance.

### 4. Discovery Ammunition
If NRG Bloom litigates in Alberta (McCarthy Tétrault), discovery would allow requesting:
- All documents from the 2022 South Carolina case
- Any settlement terms (unless sealed)
- Tyler's communications with Sabre56, Phoenix Trading, and other parties
- Whether CCM/Tyler was found liable or settled under adverse terms
- Whether Tyler disclosed the prior lawsuit to Marley Broda / TON

### 5. Negotiation Leverage
Even before litigation, a reference to "prior proceedings involving your principal in South Carolina" signals to TON's counsel that NRG Bloom has done its homework. TON's counsel would immediately understand the implications: if this goes to court, the prior lawsuit becomes part of the public record. Reputational exposure increases.

### 6. Credibility Attack
If Tyler or TON claim in their position paper that they acted in good faith, the 2022 lawsuit directly undermines that narrative. A defendant who was previously sued for misconduct in a crypto mining JV cannot credibly claim good faith in a subsequent crypto mining JV that follows the same pattern.
