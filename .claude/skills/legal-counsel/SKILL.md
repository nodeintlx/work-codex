## MODULE 1: CASE THEORY ENGINE

### Purpose
Generate, test, and refine the theory of the case — the single narrative thread that makes the decision-maker *want* to rule for NRG Bloom.

### The "Write Closing Argument First" Method
Before doing anything else on a new legal question, draft the closing argument. This forces the answer to: **"Why should we win, in one sentence?"** Everything — evidence selection, witness prep, motion strategy — is reverse-engineered from that closing.

### Three Tests for Case Theory
Every theory must pass all three:
1. **Simple enough to survive a cocktail party conversation** — if you can't explain it in 30 seconds, it's too complex
2. **Explains all uncontroverted evidence** — including the opponent's evidence, not just yours
3. **Makes the decision-maker feel that ruling for you is morally correct** — not just legally required

### Two-Layer Theory Framework
Build these separately, then align:
- **Legal theory**: The causes of action and their elements — what law was broken
- **Factual theory**: The human story — who did what, when, and why it was wrong

The factual theory is where you win or lose. The legal theory is the vehicle for delivering the factual theory to the court.

### Moral High Ground Frame
Frame every argument around **obligation owed because of a wrong committed**, not a plea for sympathy or a request for compensation. Irving Younger's tradition: moral obligation framing produces larger awards than compensation framing.

### NRG Bloom Case Theory (Current)

**Legal theory**: Breach of non-circumvention (Section 5, JVA), breach of duty of honest contractual performance (Bhasin v. Hrynew, 2014 SCC 71), breach of fiduciary duty, breach of confidence (Lac Minerals), tortious interference.

**Factual theory**: "TON used NRG Bloom to open the door to a $15M+ West Africa crypto mining opportunity, then locked NRG Bloom out and pocketed the deal — and their own CEO admitted it was calculated."

**Moral frame**: "TON made a promise, benefited from NRG Bloom's work, then deliberately betrayed their partner. This is a pattern — Tyler MacPhearson did exactly this before in South Carolina. This court should not permit that pattern of conduct to be profitable."

### Process: When Building or Refining Case Theory
1. Draft the one-sentence factual theory
2. Test it against the three criteria above
3. Identify which evidence supports the theory and which contradicts it
4. Model the opponent's counter-theory — what story will TON tell?
5. Ensure the legal theory aligns with and delivers the factual theory
6. Stress-test: could a reasonable judge hearing only the other side's story rule against you?

## MODULE 2: EVIDENCE WAR ROOM

### Purpose
Classify, deploy, and hold evidence strategically — not dump it all at once.

### Three-Tier Evidence Classification

**Tier 1 — Nuclear (hold for maximum impact)**
Evidence that, when revealed at the right moment, fundamentally undermines the opponent's entire position. Deploy in discovery responses (required), but strategically time when you *highlight* it. Reserve for cross-examination, summary judgment, or the settlement-forcing moment at mediation.

**Tier 2 — Persuasion (build the narrative)**
Evidence that confirms your story and makes it coherent. Goes into the Statement of Claim, position papers, and briefing materials as the foundation layer.

**Tier 3 — Impeachment (hold until the witness lies)**
Evidence you do NOT disclose strategically. Wait. When the witness makes a false statement at examination for discovery or trial, deploy the contradicting document. The effectiveness is multiplied by the contrast between what they said and what the document proves. If you show it early, they adjust their story.

### Staged Disclosure Strategy

Game theory confirms: timing is essential to litigation strategy. Mediocre lawyers dump everything to intimidate. Elite litigators sequence disclosure:

| Phase | What to Deploy | Why |
|-------|---------------|-----|
| Demand letter / position paper | Tier 2 persuasion evidence | Create credibility, signal strength without revealing best cards |
| Pleadings (Statement of Claim) | Plead broadly, disclose selectively | Preserve all claims, control the narrative |
| Discovery | Use Tier 3 to pin witnesses to stories | Let them commit to a version before revealing contradictions |
| Mediation / settlement | Deploy Tier 1 nuclear evidence | The moment they see their weakest exposure, they settle |
| Trial | Strongest first (opening), strongest last (closing), weakest middle | Primacy and recency effects — they remember the first and last things |

### Purpose

### Risk-Adjusted Value Formula

```
Expected Value = (Probability of Win x Best Outcome) - (Probability of Loss x Litigation Cost)
```

Calculate this for BOTH sides. The settlement zone exists where NRG Bloom's minimum is below TON's rational maximum.

## MODULE 5: BRIEF WRITING FRAMEWORK

### Purpose
Write legal documents that persuade — not just inform. Facts section is where you win or lose.

### Story First, Law Second (Bryan Garner Method)

The facts section of any brief or filing is where the case is won or lost. The legal argument section is secondary. Write the facts as a story:
1. **Protagonist** (NRG Bloom — the entrepreneur who built the door)
2. **Antagonist** (TON — the partner who walked through it and locked NRG Bloom out)
3. **Conflict** (circumvention, betrayal, pattern of predatory conduct)
4. **Resolution** (what the court can do to make it right)

### Three Pillars of Persuasion (Aristotle)

Every legal document must deploy all three:
- **Ethos** (credibility): NRG Bloom's documented expenditures, verified evidence, transparent disclosure
- **Logos** (logical chain): Timeline proves circumvention → financial model proves damages → prior lawsuit proves pattern
- **Pathos** (emotional resonance): A Canadian entrepreneur trusted a partner, contributed real money and work, and was systematically excluded

### Theme Injection

The theme is the short, compelling answer to "why should we win?" — not a legal conclusion but a moral one. Inject it into EVERY section of every document: facts, argument, and conclusion.

**NRG Bloom theme**: "TON made a promise, benefited from NRG Bloom's work, then deliberately locked out their partner — and then insulted them with a $10,000 offer. This court should not permit that pattern of conduct to be profitable."

### Argument Selection: Quality Over Quantity

A brief with three devastating arguments is stronger than a brief with ten mediocre ones. Weak arguments invite the judge to dismiss the entire filing.

**NRG Bloom's strongest three arguments** (lead with these):
1. **Breach of confidence / Lac Minerals** — doesn't require proving fiduciary relationship, evidence is overwhelming, remedy (constructive trust) is powerful
2. **Breach of duty of honest performance / Bhasin v. Hrynew** — SCC-established, TON's active concealment (cc-list removal) is textbook dishonest performance
3. **Pattern of predatory conduct / Phoenix Trading** — transforms from "he said / she said" into systemic behavior

### Word-Level Persuasion
- Choose every word in the facts section to advance the theme — no neutral language
- "Informed" vs. "concealed"; "partner" vs. "co-venturer"; "removed from correspondence" vs. "excluded from deal communications"
- Use active voice for opponent's wrongs: "TON removed NRG Bloom from the correspondence" (not "NRG Bloom was removed")
- Use passive voice for client's harm: "NRG Bloom was excluded" (emphasizes vulnerability)

### Process: Drafting Legal Documents
1. **Identify document type** — Statement of Claim, Affidavit, Motion, Brief, Position Paper
2. **Check Alberta Rules of Court** — mandatory content, form requirements (Form 10 for Statement of Claim)
3. **Write the theme first** — one sentence that answers "why should we win?"
4. **Draft the facts section as a story** — protagonist, antagonist, conflict, resolution
5. **Select 3-5 strongest legal arguments** — ruthlessly cut weak ones
6. **Ground every assertion in evidence** — reference specific exhibits from the evidence inventory
7. **Apply the three pillars** — ethos, logos, pathos in every section
8. **Review for word-level persuasion** — active/passive voice, loaded vocabulary, theme injection
9. **Verify all citations on CanLII** — mandatory, no exceptions

### Precedent Anchoring

## CROSS-EXAMINATION PREPARATION (Irving Younger Framework)

### Irving Younger's Ten Commandments of Cross-Examination

| # | Commandment | Application |
|---|-------------|-------------|
| 1 | Be brief — 3 points maximum | Identify the 3 most damaging points per witness |
| 2 | Short questions, plain words | Draft questions under 15 words |
| 3 | Leading questions only | "You removed NRG Bloom from the email on June 25, correct?" |
| 4 | Never ask what you don't know | Only ask questions where you have the contradicting document |
| 5 | Listen to the witness | Note any new admissions for follow-up |
| 6 | Do not quarrel | Stay calm — the judge sympathizes with bullied witnesses |
| 7 | Do not allow repetition of direct | Object to narrative repetition |
| 8 | Do not allow explanation | "That's a yes or no question, Mr. MacPhearson" |
| 9 | One question too many — stop before it | End the chapter BEFORE the conclusion — let the judge draw it |
| 10 | Save the conclusion for summation | Never state the devastating implication during cross — save for closing |

### Chapter Method for Cross-Examination Prep

Organize cross-examination into discrete chapters, each proving one point:
- Opens with a foundational question the witness cannot deny
- Builds through leading questions in logical sequence
- Ends BEFORE the ultimate conclusion (save for closing argument)
- Maximum 3 chapters per witness

**Chapter 1: The Promise (establish the deal)**
- "You signed a Joint Venture Agreement with NRG Bloom, correct?"
- "That agreement included a non-circumvention clause, correct?"

**Chapter 2: The Betrayal (establish the circumvention)**

## KEY FILING MECHANICS

- **Form**: Statement of Claim — Form 10
- **Court**: Court of King's Bench of Alberta
- **Filing fee**: ~$250 (verify current at alberta.ca/court-fees)
- **Filing method**: In-person for SRLs at Clerk's Office (closes 4 PM weekdays)
- **Digital filing**: KB Filing Digital Service at qb-filing.alberta.ca (check SRL eligibility)
- **Service deadline**: 1 year from filing to serve defendant
- **Litigation plan**: Required within 4 months of first Statement of Defence (new Sept 2025 rule)
- **Fee waivers**: Available at alberta.ca/waive-filing-fee

- Alberta: No specific practice direction yet — general accuracy obligations apply

- Ontario: Certification of authorities required (Dec 2024)

- **Safe practice**: Always verify on CanLII, disclose AI use proactively, maintain audit trail
