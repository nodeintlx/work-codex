Nigeria is my terrain and you can't see around the corner."*

### Phase 2: Honeymoon & Active Collaboration (August 6, 2024 - March 2025)
Ola invested $5K USDT. Both traveled to Nigeria together multiple times. Ola handled transformer procurement, logistics, security arrangements (Mopol), and customs clearing. They attended the Africa Bitcoin Conference in Nairobi together. Ola introduced the NELMCO connection via John's brother Joseph Bello. Ola received NRG Bloom email, AWS access, and camera access. Relationship was genuinely collaborative with frequent calls (often 5-10 per day), shared social outings, and mutual respect.

### Phase 3: Warning Signs (April - June 2025)
TON deal brought complexity. Ola began questioning financial transparency (Jun 12: requesting accounts). TON negotiations created frustration. Ola drafted adversarial documents toward TON. Nelson/Shugamine relationship strained. Multiple parties (Agnes, Trojan, TON) pulling in different directions. Ola started pushing for clearer returns on his investment.

### Phase 4: Deterioration (July 2025)
Triggered by discovery that Marshall may have been taking excess profits. Ola threatened to shut down transformer (Jul 6). Demanded weekly payments. Increasing hostility in messages. Accusations of financial mismanagement. However, still cooperating on container clearing for TON.

### Phase 5: Complete Breakdown (August - October 2025)
Ola demanded photo removal from website, called Makir a "grifter," questioned his LinkedIn credentials, demanded $50K buyout, threatened lawyers, used profanity, and ultimately engaged legal counsel. Communication became intermittent and hostile. Final messages deleted by Ola.

Oladipo Olaniyan ("Ola") invested approximately $5,000 USDT in NRG Bloom Inc. in August 2024, with an additional $4,000+ in transformer-related costs, under a funding agreement that also granted him a VP of Engineering title and an increased equity stake on the company's cap table. Over the following 12 months, Ola became deeply embedded in NRG Bloom's Nigerian operations -- handling transformer procurement, customs clearing, security arrangements, site logistics through his representative Yakubu Tanimu, and providing critical local contacts including a connection to Nigeria's NELMCO through his associate John's brother Joseph Bello. Despite this extensive involvement, no formalized employment contract, shareholder agreement, or clear equity documentation was ever executed beyond the initial funding agreement, leaving the precise nature and extent of Ola's rights ambiguous.

The relationship deteriorated beginning in mid-2025 when Ola discovered that Marshall (associated with Trojan Mining) may have been receiving disproportionate profits, and that NRG Bloom had not provided adequate financial accounting despite generating mining revenue. On July 6, 2025, Ola unilaterally threatened to shut down the site's transformer through his on-ground representative, and subsequently demanded $250/week in payments plus a $50,000 lump sum within 30 days to recoup his investment. He also demanded removal of his photo from NRG Bloom's website, called Makir a "grifter," questioned the legitimacy of his professional credentials, and ultimately threatened legal action through Canadian lawyers. Makir proposed a settlement restructuring Ola's position from equity to a profit-share arrangement, which Ola rejected.

The core dispute centers on: (1) whether Ola is owed a return on his investment and if so, how much and in what form; (2) the ownership status of the transformer and related equipment Ola funded; (3) Ola's claim that NRG Bloom's mining revenue was mismanaged or used as Makir's personal funds; (4) Ola's threat to physically disrupt operations by controlling the transformer through his local contacts; and (5) Ola's use of intellectual property (photos of equipment on social media) without his consent. The chat record shows a pattern of gradually escalating demands from Ola, combined with an absence of formal written agreements governing the parties' ongoing relationship, creating significant ambiguity about each party's rights and obligations.
