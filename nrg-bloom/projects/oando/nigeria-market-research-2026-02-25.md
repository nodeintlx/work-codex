## 1. Nigeria Gas Flaring Regulatory Landscape (2024–2026)

### 1.1 Nigerian Gas Flare Commercialization Programme (NGFCP)

**Current Status: Active — Execution Phase Starting 2026**

The NGFCP is Nigeria's primary market mechanism to convert flared gas into economic assets:

- **Launched:** 2016 (original), **Relaunched:** September 2022 under NUPRC
- **Structure:** Three phases — Bidding (2023), Negotiation (2024), Execution (2026+)
- **December 2025 milestone:** NUPRC issued Permits to Access Flare Gas (PAFG) to **28 frontline awardees**
- **Scale:** 49 flare sites across land, swamp, and shallow offshore; aggregate volume 250–300 mmscf/d by 2027; individual sites range from 0.5 to 30 mmscf/d
- **Investment target:** $2 billion (initial PAFG issuance); up to **$7.2 billion** projected from full programme
- **National flare-out target:** 2025 (original; now de facto 2030)
- **Eligible uses:** Power generation, LPG, petrochemicals, fertiliser, CNG

**Implication for NRG Bloom:** The 28 awardees who received PAFGs are prime partnership targets. NRG Bloom does not need its own NGFCP permit — it can partner with permit holders to provide the modular data center infrastructure powered by their allocated flare gas.

### 1.2 Petroleum Industry Act (PIA) 2021 — Gas Flaring Provisions

| Section | Provision |
|---------|-----------|
| Section 104 | Prohibits gas flaring/venting except under permitted conditions |
| Section 104(4) | Monetary penalties for unauthorized flaring |
| Section 105 | Operators must submit Gas Utilisation Plans with field development approvals |
| Section 105 | NUPRC can take gas at the flare stack **free of charge** |
| Section 108 | All licensees/lessees must submit a Flare Elimination and Monetization Plan (FEMP) |

**Key gap:** Penalties (~$0.03/MSCF historically; social cost estimated at $10.18/MSCF) remain cheaper than infrastructure investment for many operators. In the first 10 months of 2022, operators were fined **$341 million** for flaring.

**NRG Bloom's opportunity:** As enforcement tightens and penalties increase under PIA implementation, the economics of flare gas commercialization improve. NRG Bloom provides the fastest, lowest-capex path to compliance.

## 2. Gas-to-Power Market in Nigeria

### 2.1 Current Scale

Nigeria flared approximately **229 Bcf of natural gas in 2024** (World Bank). Up to 1 billion scf/day — enough to generate 5–9 GW of power. Current actual on-grid generation: ~5,639 MW against 230 million people.

### 2.2 Stranded Gas Volumes & Locations

- **NGFCP target sites:** 49 sites, land/swamp/shallow offshore in the Niger Delta
- **Site sizes:** 0.5–30 mmscf/d — well-matched to NRG Bloom's modular container model
- **Geographic concentration:** Niger Delta (Delta State, Rivers State, Bayelsa State, Imo State)

### 2.3 IPP & Captive Power Landscape

- **Electricity Act 2023** liberalized the sector significantly
- **Sub-1 MW captive generation:** No license required
- **Captive power (>1 MW):** Requires NERC permit
- **Q1 2024:** Nine new captive permits issued totaling 52.57 MW

**NRG Bloom pathway:**
1. **Captive power permit** — self-generate for on-site data center (fastest path)
2. **IPP license** — sell excess power to nearby communities (additional revenue)
3. **Mini-grid license** — power an industrial zone or host community (community relations)

## 5. Key Risks & Considerations

### 5.1 Security at Oil & Gas Sites (HIGH RISK, manageable)

| Threat | Status |
|--------|--------|
| Kidnapping for ransom | Active; 200+ foreigners taken since 2006 |
| Pipeline sabotage | Active |
| Oil theft ("bunkering") | 200,000–400,000 bbl/day lost |
| Militant groups | MEND, Niger Delta Avengers active |

**Mitigation:** Containerized infrastructure is harder to sabotage; Host Community compliance reduces grievance-driven attacks; start with land sites in established operator compounds; minimize international staff presence.

### 5.2 Local Content (NCDMB) — Non-Negotiable

- Local content level reached **56% in 2024**; targeting **70% by 2027**
- Nigerian Content Plan must be approved before any project
- All junior/intermediate staff must be Nigerian nationals
- Must involve Nigerian partners in company formation
- **Feb 2024 Presidential Directive** instructed NCDMB to balance requirements against insufficient capacity — more pragmatic enforcement

### 5.3 Foreign Exchange Risk (BIGGEST FINANCIAL THREAT)

| Issue | Data |
|-------|------|
| Naira depreciation (2024) | 40.9% decline |
| Annual inflation (2024) | 31% |
| Repatriation restriction | IOCs limited to 50% immediate repatriation |

**Mitigation:** USD-denominated contracts; offshore revenue structure; Bitcoin mining revenues are inherently USD-denominated; carbon credits are internationally tradable in USD.

### 5.4 Gas Supply Reliability

- Associated gas volumes vary with oil production rates
- Pipeline vandalism affects gas transport
- **Mitigation:** Minimum gas supply guarantees in contracts; design for 50–70% capacity utilization; battery/UPS buffer; multi-site diversification
