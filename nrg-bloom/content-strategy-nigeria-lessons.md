# Building Infrastructure in Africa: What 13 Months in Nigeria Taught Us
## Content Strategy Document — NRG Bloom

**Created**: 2026-02-26
**Purpose**: Foundation for video series and blog posts about building and operating infrastructure projects on the African continent remotely from Canada
**Author context**: Makir Volcy, CEO of NRG Bloom Inc., built a modular data center operation in Ogboinbiri, Nigeria over 13 months — managing everything from Montreal. This document captures the raw, unfiltered lessons from that experience.

---

## The Story in One Paragraph

A Canadian entrepreneur with Haitian roots and zero venture capital built a Bitcoin mining operation in a remote Nigerian community — navigating land deals with traditional rulers, customs nightmares at Lagos port, community politics, power purchase agreements, a foreign infrastructure partner who invested millions, and a 13-month relationship that ended in termination and litigation. Along the way, he learned more about building in Africa than any MBA program could teach. This is that story.

---

## PILLAR 1: THE CAPITAL TRAP
### "The Person With the Money Makes the Rules"

**The lesson**: When you bring relationships, expertise, and local operations but your partner brings the capital, the power dynamic is fundamentally unequal — no matter how essential your contribution is.

**What happened**: NRG Bloom originated the opportunity, built every local relationship, found the land, negotiated the power agreement, hired security, managed the community. The Canadian infrastructure partner brought containers, miners, and capital. Over 13 months, NRG Bloom's compensation was progressively reduced — from a signed agreement worth $9,000/month + mining equipment to an 8% hash rate share worth roughly $600/month profit. Every time NRG Bloom pushed back, the partner threatened to walk away.

**Content angles**:
- "We brought 80% of the value and ended up with 8% of the economics — here's why"
- The psychology of negotiating without capital: how the partner with the money always has the last word
- Why we operated on credit cards for months and what that does to your negotiating position
- The moment we realized: the deal we signed wasn't the deal we were promised
- How "good faith negotiations" become progressive erosion when you can't walk away

**Key quote (from actual call recording)**: *"The first deal was 250 watt miners and a $9,000 management fee. And then from then on, we just went down. We went all the way to 8%."*

**The takeaway for viewers**: If you're building in Africa without your own capital, you are building someone else's project. Get funded first — even partially. $200K of your own money changes the entire dynamic. You stop being the junior partner who has to accept whatever's offered.

## PILLAR 2: VERBAL PROMISES DON'T SURVIVE BUSINESS PRESSURE
### "If It's Not Written, It Didn't Happen"

**The lesson**: In cross-border partnerships, verbal commitments dissolve the moment business pressure arrives. Handshakes, phone promises, and "you have my word" mean nothing when millions are on the table.

**What happened**: Key promises were made verbally — equipment for our container, operations fees, inclusion in major deals, "we'll never replace you." Every single one was later denied or reframed. When confronted, the partner's response was: "every other agreement was out the window" once we met in person and discussed new terms.

**Content angles**:
- The 5 promises that were made and broken — and what they cost us
- "I give you my word" vs. "Read the contracts" — how the same partner said both
- Why African business culture (verbal, relationship-based) collides with Western legal culture (written, contractual) — and who loses
- The one-page agreement that could have saved 13 months of pain
- How to protect yourself: what every agreement with a foreign partner MUST include

**Key quote (from actual call recording)**: Partner, July 2025: *"We're not gonna go try to shop it to save 50 bucks."* Same partner, February 2026: *"We're clearing the house and bringing our guys in."*

**The takeaway for viewers**: Before you make the first introduction, before you open the first door, before you take your partner to meet the community leader — get the agreement signed. Not a handshake. Not an email. A signed document with equity protection, non-circumvention, and termination safeguards. If your partner refuses to sign before you deliver value, that tells you everything you need to know.

## PILLAR 3: MANAGING NIGERIAN STAKEHOLDERS FROM 7,000 KM AWAY
### "They Have to See Movement"

**The lesson**: In Nigeria (and much of Africa), business moves on physical presence, visible activity, and personal relationships. Managing remotely is possible but requires a completely different operating model than what works in North America.

**What happened**: NRG Bloom managed an Ogboinbiri site operation from Montreal — coordinating security guards, community relations, land payments, power agreements, and customs clearance across a 6-hour time zone gap. When the community didn't see physical progress, trust eroded. When the power provider felt ignored, she shut off the electricity. When local partners felt undervalued, disputes escalated.

**Content angles**:
- "They have to see movements" — why African business requires visible action, not just meetings and emails
- How we managed a Nigerian operation from Montreal: the daily reality
- The 3 AM phone calls, the WhatsApp management style, the constant relationship maintenance
- Building trust with a community you've only visited twice
- The local team: how to hire, manage, and retain people on the ground when you can't be there
- When your power provider shuts off the electricity because she feels disrespected — and how to recover

**Key quote (from actual call recording)**: *"In Africa, things work differently. They have to see movements. They have to see people work. They have to see things happening physically with their eyes to even believe. Meetings and talking — that's not how you translate business to them."*

**The takeaway for viewers**: You cannot run an African operation the way you run a Canadian one. Email threads and Zoom calls don't build trust in communities where business has always been face-to-face. You need a local presence — someone with authority on the ground who represents you. And you need to show progress physically, not just on paper.

## PILLAR 4: THE LOCAL PARTNER PARADOX
### "You Can't Operate Without Them — But They Can Become Your Biggest Risk"

**The lesson**: Nigerian operations require local partners — for land, for community access, for customs, for legal compliance. But informal partnerships with local stakeholders can become liabilities that foreign investors use against you.

**What happened**: NRG Bloom worked with local partners for land acquisition, community relations, and customs clearance. These relationships were essential — without them, nothing moves. But when disputes arose between local partners, the foreign infrastructure investor used the "chaos" as justification to terminate NRG Bloom entirely: "There's too much unrest. We're going to do it ourselves."

**Content angles**:
- Why you NEED Nigerian partners and what they actually provide (land access, community trust, customs navigation, regulatory compliance)
- The risks: when your local partner's interests diverge from yours
- How a foreign investor weaponized our local disputes to justify cutting us out
- Structuring local partnerships: what to formalize and what to keep flexible
- The community dynamic: traditional rulers, youth expectations, employment promises
- When your local partner demands 77% — how to negotiate when walking away isn't an option

**The takeaway for viewers**: Nigerian partners are not optional — they're the foundation. But you have to structure these relationships so that a foreign investor can't point to local complexity as a reason to bypass you. Clear roles, documented agreements, and a separation between local partnerships and your core investment partner relationship.

## PILLAR 5: CUSTOMS, LOGISTICS, AND THE NIGERIAN REALITY
### "Nothing Is Simple — But Everything Is Possible"

**The lesson**: Importing equipment into Nigeria — containers, miners, transformers — is a logistical marathon involving customs agents, port delays, unexpected fees, border holdups, barge transport through river delta waterways, and costs that routinely exceed estimates.

**What happened**: A 40-foot mining container shipped from Canada took months to clear Nigerian customs. Costs ballooned: customs clearance alone was approximately ₦23.5 million (~$15,800 USD). Unexpected fees kept surfacing — ₦5 million here, ₦3 million there. The container had to travel by barge and crane (₦15.9 million) to reach the remote Ogboinbiri site. Miners got stuck at the border. The logistics partner's estimates were consistently wrong.

**Content angles**:
- The real cost of importing a mining container into Nigeria (with actual numbers)
- Customs clearance: what they tell you it costs vs. what it actually costs
- Moving a 40-foot container by barge through the Niger Delta — the logistics nobody talks about
- "This is not how things work" — when your Canadian partner hits the Nigerian reality wall
- How we cleared customs, moved equipment, and got operational despite every obstacle
- The people who make it happen: customs agents, logistics firms, local fixers

**Key quote (from actual WhatsApp exchange)**: Foreign partner, frustrated by fees: *"I want to move forward but like this is unreal."* and *"This is not how things work."*

**The takeaway for viewers**: Budget 2-3x what you think logistics will cost. Build relationships with customs agents and logistics firms before you need them. And never, ever assume a timeline.

## PILLAR 6: THE POWER PURCHASE AGREEMENT — YOUR LIFELINE AND YOUR LEASH
### "Whoever Controls the Power Controls the Project"

**The lesson**: In off-grid or stranded-gas mining operations, the PPA (Power Purchase Agreement) is the single most important document. Whoever's name is on it controls the project. If your name isn't on it, you're expendable.

**What happened**: NRG Bloom originated the power relationship, negotiated the rate ($0.03/kWh), and maintained the provider relationship for months. But when the formal PPA was signed, NRG Bloom's name wasn't on it — the foreign partner signed directly. When the relationship ended, the partner kept the PPA and told NRG Bloom: "We are the only ones that have any sort of legal right to this."

**Content angles**:
- Why the PPA is the most important document in any African energy/mining project
- How we negotiated a $0.03/kWh rate — and then watched someone else sign it
- "We are the only ones that have legal right" — what happens when your name isn't on the PPA
- The $15.3 million question: how a 5-year PPA's true value dwarfs the equipment cost
- Protecting yourself: how to ensure your name stays on the power agreement
- The Axxela story: how we brought a $15M opportunity and were offered 0.065% of its value

**The takeaway for viewers**: If you originate a power relationship in Africa, your name MUST be on the PPA — as a co-signatory, a named operator, or at minimum referenced in binding side agreements. Without it, you built the road for someone else to drive on.

## PILLAR 7: THE EMOTIONAL TOLL — AND WHY IT MATTERS
### "Don't Speak From Your Heart — This Is Business"

**The lesson**: Building infrastructure in Africa as a diaspora entrepreneur is deeply personal. You're not just building a business — you're connecting with communities, representing something bigger, carrying cultural weight. When a partner betrays that, the emotional impact is real. But the business world will use your emotion against you.

**What happened**: After 13 months of building relationships, flying to Nigeria, paying staff from personal credit cards, and sacrificing revenue to keep the site running for the partner's benefit — the partner terminated by phone call and told us: "Don't speak from your heart. This is business. Read the contracts."

**Content angles**:
- The emotional reality of building in Africa that nobody talks about in business podcasts
- When your partner tells you "this is business, not personal" — but everything about it IS personal
- How to channel emotion into strategy: the 24-hour rule
- Julie's masterclass: how our COO stayed professional when I couldn't — and why every founder needs that person
- The moment I said "this was planned all along" — and how I turned that anger into the strongest legal case of my career
- For diaspora entrepreneurs: the unique weight of building in your ancestral continent and being told you have "no legal right" there

**Key quote (from actual call recording)**: *"How is this fair for us who worked really hard since February of last year to bring you guys there, to work with you and to manage all these challenges and to get you guys on site... And then now you're saying we're terminating you and you're just going to work with them directly and just cut us out completely."*

**The takeaway for viewers**: Your emotion is valid. But your strategy has to be cold. Document everything. Record everything. Build your evidence. And when the time comes, let the facts speak louder than your feelings. That's how you win.

## THE FORWARD VISION (Pillar 8 — For the Final Episode)

After everything — the broken promises, the progressive devaluation, the termination call, the legal fight — NRG Bloom is still building. The lessons from 13 months aren't reasons to quit. They're the blueprint for doing it right.

**What's different this time**:
1. Own capital before delivering value (grants, SR&ED, investor capital)
2. Agreements signed before introductions made
3. NRG Bloom's name on every PPA and operating agreement
4. Permanent local presence with authority
5. Every promise in writing, every call recorded, every dollar documented
6. The right legal structure from day one (Nigerian entity, Canadian parent, clear roles)

**The message**: Africa isn't the problem. Bad deals are the problem. Lack of capital is the problem. Verbal promises are the problem. The continent is full of opportunity — but you have to build the right foundation before you build the project.
