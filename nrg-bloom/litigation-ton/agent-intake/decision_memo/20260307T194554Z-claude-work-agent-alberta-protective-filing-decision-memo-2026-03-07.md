## PART 2: THE CORPORATE REPRESENTATION CONSTRAINT

### 2.1 The Problem

NRG Bloom Inc. is a corporation. Under s. 106(1) of Alberta's Legal Profession Act and Rule 2.23, **corporations cannot self-represent in Alberta Court of King's Bench.** This is not discretionary — the court cannot waive it. If NRG Bloom files without a lawyer, TON's counsel will move to strike immediately.

Cases confirming this:
- *Park Avenue Flooring Inc v EllisDon Construction Services Inc*, 2016 ABCA
- *908077 Alberta Ltd. v. 1313608 Alberta Ltd.*, 2015 ABCA 117

### 2.2 The Four Options

**Option A: Limited-Scope Alberta Retainer (RECOMMENDED)**

Retain an Alberta lawyer on a **limited-scope retainer** — they draft/file the Statement of Claim and handle formal court appearances; Makir manages strategy, evidence, and negotiation with AI support.

- Cost: $2,000-$8,000 for filing + initial appearances
- The lawyer does NOT need to run the case — they are the procedural interface
- Many Alberta firms offer unbundled legal services for exactly this
- Resources: Pro Bono Law Alberta (pbla.ca), Law Society Referral (1-800-661-1095), KB Amicus Project (Calgary — Tues/Wed/Thurs walk-in)

**Why this works:** The Statement of Claim is already drafted (391 lines, 7 causes of action, all CanLII-verified authority). The lawyer reviews, formats to Form 10, signs, and files. The hard intellectual work is done.

**Option B: Co-Plaintiff Structure**

File as co-plaintiffs: NRG Bloom Inc. (through lawyer) + Makir Volcy personally (self-represented). Makir would need personal claims — potential grounds:

- Personal time and expertise invested (13 months of operational management)
- Personal travel expenses (Montreal-Edmonton flight, Nigeria trips)
- Loss of personal income/opportunity

This gives Makir standing as a self-represented co-plaintiff while NRG Bloom has counsel. Reduces legal costs while preserving full claim scope.

**Option C: Arbitration Instead of Court**

The SDA Section 7 contains a dispute resolution mechanism. Arbitration may not have the same corporate representation restriction. However:

- Section 7 of the Arbitration Act (RSA 2000, c A-43) provides for a **mandatory stay** of court proceedings if a valid arbitration agreement exists — TON WILL invoke this
- The arbitration clause scope must be analyzed: does it cover tort claims (breach of confidence, tortious interference)? Does it cover the MNDA (a separate agreement)?
- If the arbitration clause is narrowly worded, some claims may survive the stay application
- *Uber v. Heller* (2020 SCC) allows courts to void unconscionable arbitration clauses

**Critical analysis needed:** Review the exact language of SDA Section 7 to determine scope. If it only covers "disputes arising under this Agreement," the MNDA claims, tort claims, and personal claims against Tyler may fall outside it.

**Option D: Provincial Court (Not Recommended)**

Alberta Provincial Court allows non-lawyer corporate representation — but the monetary limit is effectively $100K. NRG Bloom's claim exceeds $600K. This would require abandoning the majority of the claim. Not viable.

### 2.3 Recommended Path

**Option A + Option B combined.** Retain a limited-scope Alberta lawyer for NRG Bloom Inc. Structure the action with Makir Volcy as a co-plaintiff for personal claims. The lawyer handles formal procedural requirements; Makir and the AI litigation platform handle strategy, evidence, and negotiation.

## PART 3: FORUM ANALYSIS — ALBERTA vs. NIGERIA vs. BOTH

### 3.1 Why Alberta Works

- TON is an Alberta corporation — presumptive jurisdiction under the SCC's *Club Resorts* test
- The MNDA expressly selects Alberta law and Alberta courts
- Marley Broda personally invited NRG Bloom to litigate in Alberta — this is on the record and estops any forum non conveniens argument
- An Alberta judgment is directly enforceable against TON's Alberta assets — no cross-border enforcement needed

### 3.2 The Mandatory Stay Risk

If the SDA's Section 7 arbitration clause is broad, TON will apply for a stay under s. 7 of the Arbitration Act. The court MUST grant the stay unless:

1. The arbitration agreement is void, inoperative, or incapable of being performed
2. The clause is unconscionable (*Uber v. Heller*)
3. The claims fall outside the scope of the arbitration agreement

**Mitigation strategy:** Structure the Statement of Claim to emphasize claims that fall OUTSIDE the SDA:
- **MNDA breach** — separate agreement, separate governing law clause, may have its own dispute resolution mechanism (or none)
- **Tort claims** — breach of confidence (LAC Minerals), fraudulent misrepresentation, tortious interference — these are independent of contract
- **Personal claims against Tyler** — he is not a party to the SDA
- **Fiduciary duty** — equitable obligation, not dependent on any single contract

Even if TON gets a partial stay on SDA-specific claims, the MNDA claims, tort claims, and personal claims against Tyler may proceed in court.

### 3.3 Relationship to Nigerian Track

Per the Codex handoff, Claim C1 (bad-faith termination) is on the `nigeria_primary` track, while Claim C2 (Axxela circumvention) is on the `canada_or_cross_border` track. This is correct. The Alberta filing focuses on:

- C3 (misrepresentation to Axxela) — Alberta tort claim
- New: Tyler's personal liability — Alberta defendant, Alberta personal service
- New: Breach of confidence (LAC Minerals) — Alberta equitable claim

The Nigerian track handles C1 (bad-faith termination under SDA) and C4 (unjust enrichment for operational work). The two tracks are complementary, not competing. Filing in Alberta does not prejudice the Nigerian process — it amplifies pressure by opening a second front.
