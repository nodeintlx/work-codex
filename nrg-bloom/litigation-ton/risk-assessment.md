## I. TON'S DEFENSE STRATEGY -- DECODED

The 14-business-day scheduling proposal is strategic, not lazy. Every month that passes costs NRG Bloom money in legal fees while TON continues operating its other projects. They are betting NRG Bloom can't afford a prolonged fight across two jurisdictions. If the 30-day negotiation window expires with nothing accomplished, then it's 45 days of mediation, then arbitration. Pushing Axxela circumvention to Alberta isn't just a legal argument -- it's a financial weapon. If NRG Bloom has to litigate MNDA claims in Alberta AND Agreement claims in Nigeria, it needs lawyers in both jurisdictions. That could double litigation costs. They may be calculating that the cost of two-front litigation exceeds the settlement value for a company NRG Bloom's size.

### Layer 3: Contractual Ceiling
If forced to the table, their fallback is: "The May 16 Agreement is the deal. You agreed to 8% + $875/month. Even if we owe you that, it's a small number." They will try to cap damages at what the contract says, which is far below ₦1B.

### Layer 4: Nuisance Settlement
If all else fails, offer $50K-$100K as a "go away" payment, knowing that enforcing a larger judgment cross-border is expensive and uncertain. They may calculate that Makir would take a sure $75K over a theoretical $600K that takes two years and $150K in legal fees to collect.

## II. GENUINE CHALLENGES TO GETTING ₦1B ($606K)

### Challenge 1: The May 16 Agreement Likely Supersedes the JV Agreement

This is the biggest structural risk. The JV Agreement's exclusivity clause (Section 5) and 90-day termination provision (Section 8) are powerful -- but if the May 16 Agreement contains an "entire agreement" clause (which most professionally drafted agreements do), it replaces all prior agreements. TON will argue:

- *"The JV Agreement is dead. The parties negotiated a new agreement that superseded it. The exclusivity clause doesn't exist anymore."*

If that argument holds, the exclusivity clause can't be used to bring circumvention claims under Nigerian jurisdiction, and the 90-day notice period is irrelevant.

**Action required:** Dayo must check the May 16 Agreement for an entire agreement / superseding clause immediately. If it's there, the JV Agreement becomes background evidence of the devaluation pattern -- still useful contextually, but not an independent legal basis for claims.

### Challenge 2: The Duress Argument Is Hard to Win

Courts set a high bar for economic duress. TON will argue:

- Makir voluntarily flew to Edmonton
- He stayed at Marley's home for 10 days -- that looks like friendship and collaboration, not coercion
- He had time to consult a lawyer before signing
- He signed the May 16 Agreement without writing "under protest" or any formal objection
- He performed for 9 more months under those terms without filing a legal challenge

The legal standard usually requires: (1) an illegitimate threat, (2) no reasonable alternative, and (3) the threat caused the agreement.

- **#2 is strong:** NRG Bloom had turned down Marshall Long, had sunk costs, had non-refundable flights, and was commercially trapped.
- **#1 is supported:** The "calculated tactic" admission helps establish the threat was illegitimate.
- **#3 is where it gets tricky:** The 10 days at Marley's house looks like collaborative negotiation to an outside observer, not someone signing under duress. The absence of any contemporaneous written protest is a gap.

### Challenge 3: The "Calculated Tactic" Is Oral Testimony Against Denial

Tyler will deny it, reframe it, or claim it was taken out of context. NRG Bloom has two witnesses (Makir + Julie) -- but they are both NRG Bloom principals, so a tribunal might discount it as self-serving testimony. There is no recording, no email, no text message where Tyler wrote those words. Julie's affidavit helps, but it's not a smoking gun the way a written admission would be.

**Compare this to the Axxela circumvention evidence**, which is documented in black and white across dozens of emails. The circumvention claim is the strongest precisely because it doesn't depend on anyone's memory.

### Challenge 4: Damages Quantification Is the Weakest Area

NRG Bloom's demand letter claims ₦1.55B-₦4B. The quantification challenge:

| Damages Theory | Challenge |
|---|---|
| **Lost revenue share** | At 8% + $875/month, even over several years this doesn't reach $606K |
| **Axxela opportunity value** | The $15.3M five-year deal hasn't materialized. It's speculative. An arbitrator may discount future projected revenue heavily, especially for a deal never signed. |
| **Unjust enrichment / quantum meruit** | Requires quantifying the reasonable market value of 13 months of full-service operations management. This is subjective -- TON will argue it was worth far less. |
| **JV Agreement terms as benchmark** | 1MW + $9K/month is roughly $108K/year in ops fees plus mining revenue. Over 13 months that's maybe $120K-$200K depending on BTC price. Still far below ₦1B. |

**The gap between what's provable and what's demanded is the biggest risk.** An arbitrator might award $150K-$250K based on proven damages and call it fair. TON knows this, which is why they're comfortable rejecting the full demand.

### Challenge 5: Collection Risk

Even with a favorable Nigerian arbitration award of ₦1B:

- **TON is a Canadian company with no known Nigerian assets.** The containers and equipment at Ogboinbiri are TON's, but seizing them in rural Nigeria is practically difficult.
- **Enforcing a Nigerian arbitral award in Canada** requires domesticating it through Canadian courts. Canada is a signatory to the New York Convention, so it's possible -- but TON will challenge enforcement, adding another layer of legal costs and delay.
- **TON's financial capacity.** If TON's assets are tied up in the 87MW Texas project and Canadian operations, is there liquid cash to collect? A judgment that can't be collected is just paper.
- **Corporate structure.** If TON Infrastructure Ltd. is thinly capitalized and real assets sit in other entities controlled by Tyler and Marley, piercing the corporate veil is extremely difficult under Canadian law.

### Challenge 6: The Safety Defense Has Some Teeth

The electrical issues appear to be real. Jeff's December analysis identified specific technical problems (undersized conductors, non-compliant overcurrent protection). An arbitrator -- especially one with engineering sensibility -- might view those concerns as legitimate even if the timing was suspicious. TON can argue:

*"We discovered serious safety issues, we gave NRG Bloom deadlines to address them, NRG Bloom failed to meet those deadlines, and we terminated to protect people's lives."*

That narrative has emotional weight regardless of the surrounding context. NRG Bloom's counter (manufactured pretext, Christmas deadlines, refused to fund the fixes) is strong but requires the arbitrator to see the bigger picture rather than focusing on the technical safety issues in isolation.

### Challenge 7: The Affirmation Problem

13 months of performance without a formal legal challenge is a long time. Makir BCC'd Dayo in July 2025 -- but that's private. From TON's perspective, NRG Bloom continued performing for 7 more months after the May 16 Agreement. Makir pushed back informally (April confrontation, July Axxela objection), but never in the form of a formal written notice saying *"I consider this agreement to be under duress and I reserve my rights."*

The strongest counter is that the duress was continuous (ongoing termination threats) and Makir had no practical alternative. But an arbitrator could reasonably find that 13 months of willing performance constitutes affirmation.

They are not hoping NRG Bloom drops the case. They are hoping the **math of litigation** forces a settlement for significantly less than ₦1B.

## V. RECOMMENDED COUNTER-STRATEGY

### Move Fast, Keep Costs Low, Use Axxela as the Real Lever

The honest risk is that an arbitrator awards something in the $200K-$400K range rather than ₦1B, and collection adds another $50K-$100K in costs and 6-12 months. TON's strategy is to make litigation more expensive than the settlement, forcing a discount.

**The counter-strategy has three prongs:**

1. **Speed:** Push for immediate negotiation (within 7 days). Don't let TON burn the 30-day window. Show readiness for mediation and arbitration. The faster this moves, the less the delay strategy works.

2. **Cost efficiency:** Keep proceedings consolidated in Nigeria where possible. Use the JV exclusivity clause (if it survives the "entire agreement" analysis) to keep circumvention claims in Nigerian jurisdiction. Avoid Alberta proceedings unless strategically necessary.

3. **Axxela leverage:** TON can live with paying $200K in a settlement. They cannot live with losing the $15.3M Axxela opportunity. An injunction threat -- or even NRG Bloom reaching out to Axxela directly with the circumvention evidence -- is the real pressure point. The question isn't "can we win $606K in arbitration?" The question is "can we make it more expensive for TON to fight than to pay?"

### Key Strategic Decisions for Dayo

1. **Check the May 16 Agreement for an "entire agreement" clause** -- this determines whether the JV exclusivity clause has independent legal force or is only contextual evidence
2. **Evaluate the Axxela injunction option** -- what would it take to freeze TON's Axxela engagement pending resolution? This is the highest-leverage move available
3. **Set a realistic internal floor** -- given the quantification challenges and collection risks, what is the true minimum acceptable outcome? Build the negotiation strategy around that number, not the demand letter figure
4. **Decide when to reveal the new evidence** -- the JV Agreement and Axxela email trail are informational advantages. Deploy during negotiation to shock TON into a higher settlement, or hold for arbitration where it has maximum evidentiary impact?
