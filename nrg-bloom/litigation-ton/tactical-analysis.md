### Argument 1: Corporate Identity (NRG Bloom Inc. vs. NRG Bloom Services Limited)

**What TON says:** NRG Bloom Inc. (Quebec) and NRG Bloom Services Limited (Nigeria) are different legal entities. Which one is claiming what?

**Strength:** Medium. This is a procedural challenge, not a substantive defense. It creates complexity but doesn't defeat the claims.

**Vulnerability:**
- TON signed the Agreement with NRG Bloom Inc. That's the contracting party.
- NRG Bloom Services Limited was created to operationalize the Nigerian side -- a standard corporate structure for foreign entities operating in Nigeria
- TON benefited from the services regardless of which entity delivered them
- The fact that Moroom Africa's address matches NRG Bloom Services Limited is coincidental (shared office space is common in Lagos)
- **If TON argues the Nigerian entity is separate, they weaken their own counterclaims against NRG Bloom Inc.** -- they can't have it both ways
- TON's own response at 2.1.4 concedes the point: they acknowledge "any services rendered by Nigerian-incorporated personnel were done pursuant to NRG Bloom Inc.'s obligations under the Agreement" -- effectively admitting the agency relationship while pretending to challenge it

**Action for Dayo:**
- Clarify the corporate relationship (principal-subsidiary or agency)
- Confirm NRG Bloom Inc. is the proper claimant as the contracting party
- NRG Bloom Services Limited operated as NRG Bloom Inc.'s authorized agent in Nigeria

### Argument 2: MNDA Jurisdiction Split (Alberta vs. Nigeria)

**What TON says:** The MNDA is governed by Alberta law, so circumvention claims must go to Alberta courts, not Nigerian arbitration.

**Strength:** High on paper. The MNDA does specify Alberta jurisdiction. This is a legitimate jurisdictional argument.

**Vulnerability:**
- The circumvention is also a breach of the **Agreement itself**, not just the MNDA. Section 5 of the NDA states compensation for introductions must be negotiated before either party enters arrangements with introduced parties. The Agreement incorporated the spirit of non-circumvention.
- **NEW EVIDENCE -- JV Agreement Exclusivity Clause:** The original JV Agreement (Feb 19, 2025) contains Section 5: *"TON agrees that all current and future Bitcoin mining projects in Nigeria will be done exclusively with NRG BLOOM."* If TON is pursuing Axxela's $15.3M opportunity without NRG Bloom, that is a breach of this exclusivity commitment. This claim can be brought under the Agreement (Nigerian jurisdiction), not just the MNDA. This neutralizes the jurisdiction-splitting strategy.
- The Axxela circumvention directly relates to the Ogboinbiri project and the Agreement -- it is "arising out of or in connection with" the Agreement, bringing it within Section 7's scope.
- TON's circumvention behavior is relevant as **evidence of bad faith** under the Agreement, even if the standalone MNDA claim goes to Alberta.
- **Splitting jurisdiction increases TON's legal costs and exposure.** They now face potential proceedings in Alberta AND Nigeria.
- The circumvention evidence (Marley's false email to Franklin on Jul 4, 2025) remains admissible as evidence of TON's character and pattern of conduct.

**Action for Dayo:**
- Frame circumvention claims under BOTH the MNDA (Alberta) AND as breach of the JV Agreement's exclusivity clause and the Agreement's implied duties (Nigeria)
- Use the JV Agreement's Section 5 exclusivity language to keep circumvention claims in Nigerian jurisdiction
- Use circumvention evidence as proof of bad faith under the Agreement, separate from the MNDA claim
- If necessary, commence parallel Alberta proceedings for MNDA-specific relief
- **This actually increases TON's exposure** -- two jurisdictions, two sets of legal costs

### Argument 3: Section 9 -- Unconditional Termination Right

**What TON says:** Section 9 gives TON the right to terminate on 30 days' notice at any time. This is unconditional. The termination was lawful.

**Strength:** This is TON's strongest argument. If Section 9 truly provides unconditional termination, the "wrongful termination" claim is weakened.

**Vulnerability:**
- **NEW EVIDENCE -- JV Agreement Termination Terms:** The original JV Agreement (Feb 19, 2025) required **90 days' written notice** with a **30-day cure period** (Section 8). The May 16, 2025 Agreement reduced this to 30 days' notice. But even under the reduced terms, **TON gave zero days' notice.** Jeff Jespersen said on Feb 9: *"We're going to take this away, digest it, and get back to you on how we will navigate our next steps."* Three days later, termination. That is 3 days, not 30.
- **An unconditional termination right does not extinguish liability for damages caused by bad faith conduct leading up to termination.** TON can have the right to terminate AND still owe damages for unjust enrichment, circumvention, and economic duress that occurred during the relationship.
- The termination right doesn't shield TON from claims for **value already conferred** (quantum meruit) or for the pattern of extractive behavior documented over 13 months.
- Under Nigerian law, even unconditional termination rights are subject to the implied duty of good faith. A party cannot use a termination right as a weapon to extract concessions.
- **The timing is the evidence.** TON terminated at the precise moment when:
  - NRG Bloom's revenue share was about to begin (containers deployed, transformer installed)
  - NRG Bloom had accepted $36K-$59K in electrical remediation costs
  - Canurta had been introduced as an institutional equity partner (Jan 16, 2026)
  - NRG Bloom had committed to purchasing a new custom 40ft container
- Section 9 may require return of NRG Bloom's investment and fair compensation -- review the exact language
- **Tyler admitted the first termination was a "calculated tactic."** This admission colors all subsequent termination threats and the final termination.
- **The progression from 90-day to 30-day to 0-day notice is itself evidence of the devaluation pattern.** Each iteration eroded NRG Bloom's protections.

**Action for Dayo:**
- Review Section 9 of the May 16 Agreement and confirm the 30-day notice requirement
- Argue TON failed to provide the required 30 days' notice (Jeff's Feb 9 email proves no warning was given)
- Argue that unconditional termination does not bar damages for pre-termination conduct
- Focus on unjust enrichment and quantum meruit (value conferred) rather than wrongful termination per se
- Use "calculated tactic" admission to establish pattern -- the final termination is the same pattern weaponized
- Show the erosion from 90-day → 30-day → 0-day as evidence of progressive rights-stripping

### Argument 4: Section 5 -- Discontinuation Right

**What TON says:** TON has "absolute right, exercisable in its sole discretion, to discontinue participation in the project after the completion of any phase" with no damages owed beyond existing revenue sharing.

**Strength:** High if enforceable as written. This would bar most damages claims.

**Vulnerability:**
- **"After the completion of any phase"** -- which phase was completed? The 40ft container never became operational. The transformer was installed but not connected. The site was shut down before operations began. TON terminated DURING a phase, not after completing one.
- Even if the clause is enforceable, it contemplates "existing revenue sharing from completed operations." NRG Bloom was operating the 20ft container and earning a 33% hashrate allocation for 5 weeks. That revenue sharing was "existing" and "completed."
- **Unconscionability argument:** If Section 5 allows TON to consume 13 months of NRG Bloom's labor, introductions, and operational management, then walk away paying nothing, that clause is unconscionable and unenforceable.
- The clause may have been procured under economic duress (the progressive devaluation pattern).
- Under Nigerian law, limitation clauses are unenforceable where fundamental breach has occurred. TON's circumvention of the MNDA, false representations to Axxela, and fabrication of termination pretexts constitute fundamental breach.
- **NEW EVIDENCE -- JV Agreement shows the original deal had no such escape clause.** The Section 5 discontinuation right appears only in the May 16 Agreement, negotiated under duress after Tyler's "calculated tactic" termination threat. The original JV Agreement did not contain this provision. The clause was inserted when NRG Bloom was in a weakened negotiating position (non-refundable flights, 10 days at Marley's home).

**Action for Dayo:**
- Challenge the "completion of any phase" language -- TON terminated mid-phase
- Argue unconscionability and fundamental breach
- Show the discontinuation clause was absent from the original JV Agreement and only appeared in the duress-negotiated May 16 Agreement
- If Section 5 bars contract damages, pivot entirely to quantum meruit and unjust enrichment (which exist independently of the contract)

### Argument 5: Affirmation Defeats Duress

**What TON says:** NRG Bloom performed for 13 months without formally objecting to duress, so it affirmed the contract and waived duress claims.

**Strength:** Medium-high. This is a technically valid legal defense.

**Vulnerability:**
- **NRG Bloom had no practical alternative.** Having invested substantial time, money, relationships, and having turned down Marshall Long (its sole revenue source), NRG Bloom was commercially trapped. Continuing to perform doesn't equal free consent -- it equals having no choice.
- **The duress was ongoing.** Each new termination threat renewed the coercion. The threats continued through July, September, and December 2025. You can't "affirm" a contract while the duress is still active.
- **NRG Bloom DID object.** Makir pushed back on multiple occasions (April 21 confrontation, July 11 Axxela rejection, December 24 criticism). He BCC'd his lawyer as early as July 2025.
- The affirmation defense applies when duress ceases and the party has a genuine choice. NRG Bloom never had that luxury.

**Action for Dayo:**
- Document the continuous nature of the duress (threat timeline)
- Show NRG Bloom's lack of practical alternatives (turned down Marshall, sunk costs, relationship dependencies)
- Point to the July 2025 lawyer engagement as evidence NRG Bloom was actively seeking legal recourse, not affirming

### Argument 6: Quantum Meruit Barred by Express Contract

**What TON says:** You can't claim quantum meruit when an express contract already governs compensation. NRG Bloom agreed to 8% revenue + $875/month.

**Strength:** Medium. Legally correct as a general principle.

**Vulnerability:**
- **The contract has been terminated.** Once TON terminated, the contractual compensation structure ceased. Quantum meruit applies to recover the reasonable value of services already rendered where the contract no longer governs.
- **The contract was procured under duress.** If the duress claim succeeds, the devalued terms are voidable, and quantum meruit fills the gap.
- **The contract didn't compensate for all services.** NRG Bloom's introductions, opportunity origination, and Nigeria market entry facilitation are not covered by the 8% revenue share, which only relates to the Ogboinbiri site operations. The broader value creation is outside the contract's scope.
- **NEW EVIDENCE -- The original JV Agreement valued NRG Bloom's contribution at 1MW allocation + $9,000/month.** The market rate for NRG Bloom's services (as agreed before duress) was approximately 10x what the May 16 Agreement provided. This establishes the reasonable value benchmark for quantum meruit.
- **Unjust enrichment is the stronger parallel claim.** Even if QM is technically barred, unjust enrichment based on the same facts remains available under Nigerian law.

**Action for Dayo:**
- Argue QM applies post-termination (contract no longer governs)
- Argue QM applies to services outside the contract's scope (introductions, opportunity origination)
- Use the original JV Agreement terms as the benchmark for "reasonable value" of services
- Alternatively, reframe as unjust enrichment (slightly different legal basis, same result)
