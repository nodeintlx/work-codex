## 6. THE TERMINATION

### Events Leading Up to the Termination

**October-November 2025:** Project nearing operational status. Containers in Bayelsa, transformer delivered, electrical work progressing.

**December 24-30, 2025:** Makir's last substantive messages go largely unanswered. His warning about operational friction and communication failures is ignored.

**January 2026:** Complete silence. Per the Ola CONTEXT.md: "In January 2026, Ola's agents physically locked the container and transformer, shutting down the site entirely -- an act that directly caused TON Infrastructure, NRG Bloom's most important strategic partner, to terminate their 5MW Revenue Sharing Agreement."

**February 2, 2026:** Last message in chat -- casual, unrelated to business.

**February 12, 2026:** TON terminates the agreement.

### Warning Signs

1. **Communication deterioration** -- From daily interactions in November to nothing in January
2. **Refusal to pay management fee** -- Signaling disengagement
3. **Tyler going silent** -- Key technical partner stops responding
4. **"Not sure this site needs to get online"** -- Marley's December 4 response to management fee request
5. **No discussion of resolution** when Ola threats were known
6. **Marley's casual indifference** to Ola contacting TON: "ya doesn't matter to us if he talks to us"

### Was the Termination Justified or Opportunistic?

**Evidence it was opportunistic:**

1. **TON knew about the Ola risk for months.** As early as August 25, 2025, the chat documents awareness that "Everyone is concerned that Ola is holding that container as leverage." Makir was actively managing this risk through legal channels.

2. **TON never proposed solutions.** At no point did Marley suggest alternative approaches to the Ola problem, offer legal support, or suggest a contingency plan.

3. **TON had already extracted maximum value from NRG Bloom.** All of NRG Bloom's connections (Agnes, community, Nelson, site access) were now directly accessible to TON. TON's infrastructure was on the ground.

4. **TON was already pulling back before January.** The December communication breakdown -- refusing management fees, Tyler not returning calls, no response to Makir's operational concerns -- suggests the decision to disengage had already been made.

5. **No attempt at remediation.** A partner acting in good faith would have discussed the Ola lockdown, explored solutions, or at minimum communicated before terminating a year-long partnership.

6. **The site was about to become profitable.** With containers deployed, transformer installed, electrical nearly complete, and 95 miners ready to hash -- the project was at the inflection point where NRG Bloom's revenue share would begin. Terminating at this exact moment maximizes TON's benefit and eliminates NRG Bloom's upside.
