## D. TON'S LEGAL ARGUMENTS — VULNERABILITY ASSESSMENT

### D.1 Section 9: "Unconditional Right to Terminate" (§2.3)
**Their argument**: Section 9 gives TON the right to terminate on 30 days' notice. Period. No preconditions.

**Vulnerabilities**:
- Even if termination is contractually "lawful," the MANNER and PURPOSE can still give rise to claims
- A termination exercised in bad faith (to appropriate the other party's relationships and work product) can constitute a breach of the implied duty of good faith under Nigerian law
- The termination was used to capture NRG Bloom's Axxela relationship and site operations — this goes beyond "discontinuing participation"
- Section 9 doesn't grant the right to circumvent the MNDA's non-compete provisions
- **New evidence**: The Feb 12 recording shows Jeff explicitly stated the purpose: "bring in different guys, we're just do it ourselves" — confirming the termination was a vehicle for appropriation, not a legitimate business decision

### D.2 Section 5: "Absolute Right to Discontinue" (§2.3.3)
**Their argument**: TON can discontinue after any phase without breach, and NRG Bloom has no damages claim beyond existing revenue sharing.

**Vulnerabilities**:
- Section 5 is about phased participation in the Ogboinbiri project — it doesn't cover the Axxela deal (a completely separate opportunity)
- Even if it limits Ogboinbiri damages, it cannot protect TON from MNDA circumvention claims on the Axxela opportunity
- The Axxela 10MW deal is worth $15.3M over 5 years — entirely outside Section 5's scope
- A limitation clause doesn't survive fundamental breach (circumvention, misrepresentation)

### D.3 Jurisdiction Split: MNDA → Alberta, Agreement → Nigeria (§2.2)
**Their argument**: Non-circumvention claims under the MNDA must go to Alberta courts, not Nigerian arbitration.

**Vulnerabilities**:
- This is a delay/cost tactic — they want NRG Bloom to fight on two fronts
- But the circumvention conduct also constitutes a breach of good faith under the Nigerian Agreement itself — not just the MNDA
- The misrepresentation to Axxela (Marley's July 4 email) is actionable under Nigerian tort law regardless of the MNDA
- A mediator/arbitrator may view this procedural splitting as bad faith
- **Practical counter**: Dayo can argue the circumvention is relevant context for the Agreement dispute even if MNDA claims are technically Alberta-governed

### D.4 Corporate Identity: NRG Bloom Inc. vs NRG Bloom Services Ltd (§2.1)
**Their argument**: Nigerian staff were employed by NRG Bloom Services Limited (Nigeria), not NRG Bloom Inc. (Quebec). Claims can't be attributed across entities.

**Vulnerabilities**:
- This is a technical/procedural defense, not a factual one
- TON dealt with Makir as the principal throughout — they never cared about corporate structure until litigation
- The WhatsApp chats, emails, and call transcripts all show TON treated "NRG Bloom" and "Makir/Julie" as interchangeable
- If the Nigerian entity operated as an agent of NRG Bloom Inc. (which it obviously did), the veil argument collapses
- **Notably**: TON admits the Nigerian entity is at the SAME ADDRESS as Moroom Africa (Dayo's firm) — this isn't a shell company, it's a properly structured local operating entity

### D.5 Quantum Meruit Defense (§4.3)
**Their argument**: Can't claim quantum meruit when a contract exists. Cites Aluminium Manufacturing Co. v. Nigerian Ports Authority.

**Vulnerabilities**:
- This is actually their strongest legal argument
- However, if the contract is found to have been procured through duress or vitiated by bad faith, quantum meruit can apply to services rendered outside the contract's scope
- The Axxela-related work (introduction, relationship building, negotiations over 5 months) is NOT covered by the May 16 Agreement — so quantum meruit applies to that separately
- NRG Bloom's site development work before the May 16 Agreement (Feb–May 2025) also falls outside the contract

### D.6 Affirmation/Duress Defense (§3.1.4)
**Their argument**: NRG Bloom performed under the contract for 13 months without objecting — therefore it affirmed the agreement and waived any duress claim.

**Vulnerabilities**:
- NRG Bloom DID object — repeatedly. The WhatsApp record shows:
  - Apr 11: Makir raises circumvention concern [entry #10]
  - Jul 18: Makir's detailed economic objection ($1K/MW = 0.065%) [entry #29]
  - Dec 24: Makir's formal complaint about communication breakdown [entry #50]
- The duress was continuous — Tyler's termination threats came in waves (Apr 21, May 12), making it ongoing rather than a one-time event
- Performing under protest is not affirmation — especially when the alternative is losing everything

### D.7 Electrical/Safety Concerns as Termination Justification (§3.4.3)
**Their argument**: December 2025 compliance review found "specific and serious engineering violations" — this contributed to termination.

**Vulnerabilities**:
- On the June 24 call, David Billay himself proposed upgrading the container's electrical — meaning TON KNEW about the container's specs and was planning upgrades [T3-B]
- Tyler on the same call agreed to provide PDUs, cables, switches for the upgrade
- TON can't claim they discovered electrical issues in December when they'd been planning electrical upgrades since June
- The "30 ASIC miners placed without approval" claim — needs to be checked against the WhatsApp record. Were TON personnel involved in or aware of this?
- This reads as a post-hoc justification manufactured for litigation
